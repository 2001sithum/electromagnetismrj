Stoke's Theorem and Conservative Fields

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q3a): Using Stoke's theorem, prove that the curl of the gradient of a scalar (\(\Phi\)) is zero.

Ultra-Detailed Derivation of \( \nabla \times (\nabla \Phi) = 0 \)

Step 1: Define a Conservative Field.

A vector field \( \vec{A} \) is called conservative if it can be expressed as the gradient of a scalar potential field \( \Phi \). Let's define our field this way:

$$ \vec{A} = \nabla \Phi $$
Step 2: Recall the Fundamental Theorem for Gradients.

This theorem states that the line integral of a gradient field between two points, P and Q, depends only on the values of the scalar potential at those endpoints, not the path taken.

$$ \int_P^Q (\nabla \Phi) \cdot d\vec{l} = \Phi(Q) - \Phi(P) $$
Step 3: Apply the theorem to a closed path.

For any closed path \( C \), the starting point P is the same as the ending point Q. Therefore, the line integral of our conservative field \( \vec{A} \) around this closed path must be zero.

$$ \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \oint_C (\nabla \Phi) \cdot d\vec{l} = \Phi(P) - \Phi(P) = 0 $$

This is a defining property: the circulation of a conservative field is always zero.

Step 4: State Stoke's Theorem.

Stoke's theorem provides a relationship between a line integral around a closed loop C and a surface integral over any surface S that has C as its boundary:

$$ \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{S} $$
Step 5: Combine the results.

From Step 3, we know the left-hand side of Stoke's theorem is zero for our field \( \vec{A} = \nabla \Phi \). Therefore, the right-hand side must also be zero.

$$ 0 = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{S} $$

Substituting \( \vec{A} = \nabla \Phi \) back in gives:

$$ \int_S (\nabla \times (\nabla \Phi)) \cdot d\vec{S} = 0 $$
Step 6: The Final Conclusion.

The equation from Step 5 must hold true for *any* choice of closed path C, which means it must hold for *any* corresponding surface S. If the integral of a continuous vector field over every possible surface is zero, then the vector field itself must be identically zero at every point.

$$ \nabla \times (\nabla \Phi) = 0 $$

Future Exam Preparation: Model Question

Question: Using the differential forms of the Ampere-Maxwell Law and Gauss's Law for Electricity, provide an ultra-detailed derivation of the equation of continuity (\( \nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \partial\rho/\partial t = 0 \)).

Ultra-Detailed Model Answer

Step 1: Start with the differential form of the Ampere-Maxwell Law. $$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0\vec{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $$
Step 2: Take the divergence of every term in the equation.

The divergence is a linear operator, so we can apply it to each term individually.

$$ \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{B}) = \nabla \cdot (\mu_0\vec{J}) + \nabla \cdot (\mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}) $$
Step 3: Apply the vector calculus identity: "The divergence of the curl is always zero."

For any vector field \( \vec{F} \), it is a mathematical identity that \( \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = 0 \). Therefore, the left-hand side of our equation is zero.

$$ 0 = \nabla \cdot (\mu_0\vec{J}) + \nabla \cdot (\mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}) $$
Step 4: Pull constants out of the divergence and commute operators.

The constants \( \mu_0 \) and \( \varepsilon_0 \) can be pulled outside the divergence operator. The spatial divergence and the partial time derivative are independent and can be swapped.

$$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) $$
Step 5: Substitute the differential form of Gauss's Law for Electricity.

Gauss's Law is \( \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0 \). We substitute this into the equation from Step 4.

$$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0\varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \right) $$
Step 6: Simplify the expression.

Inside the time derivative, the constant \( 1/\varepsilon_0 \) can be pulled out, cancelling with the \( \varepsilon_0 \) in front.

$$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0 \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} $$

Finally, divide the entire equation by the non-zero constant \( \mu_0 \).

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} = 0 $$

This is the equation of continuity, which represents the local conservation of electric charge.
