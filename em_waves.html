Part 3: Electromagnetic Waves

One of the most profound predictions of Maxwell's equations is the existence of self-propagating electromagnetic waves. This derivation shows how the wave equation naturally emerges from the fundamental laws of E&M.

Deriving the Wave Equation in Free Space

We consider "free space," which means there are no charges (\( \rho = 0 \)) and no currents (\( \vec{J} = 0 \)).

Derivation for the Electric Field (\( \vec{E} \))

Step 1: Start with Maxwell's equations in free space.
  1. \( \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0 \)
  2. \( \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 \)
  3. \( \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \) (Faraday's Law)
  4. \( \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \) (Ampere-Maxwell Law)
Step 2: Take the curl of Faraday's Law (Eq. 3). $$ \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = \nabla \times \left(- \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}\right) $$ $$ \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \times \vec{B}) $$
Step 3: Apply the vector identity \( \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{A}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{A}) - \nabla^2 \vec{A} \) to the left side. $$ \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) - \nabla^2 \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \times \vec{B}) $$
Step 4: Substitute Eq. 1 (\( \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0 \)) into the left side and Eq. 4 (\( \nabla \times \vec{B} \)) into the right side. $$ \nabla(0) - \nabla^2 \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(\mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}\right) $$ $$ - \nabla^2 \vec{E} = - \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} $$
Result: This simplifies to the wave equation for the electric field.
$$ \nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} $$

A similar derivation, starting with the curl of the Ampere-Maxwell law, yields the same wave equation for the magnetic field: \( \nabla^2 \vec{B} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{B}}{\partial t^2} \)

The Speed of Light

The standard form of a 3D wave equation is \( \nabla^2 \psi = \frac{1}{v^2} \frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial t^2} \), where \( v \) is the wave speed.

By comparing our derived equation to the standard form, we can identify the speed of electromagnetic waves:

$$ \frac{1}{v^2} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \quad \implies \quad v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} $$

Plugging in the known values for the permeability of free space (\( \mu_0 \)) and the permittivity of free space (\( \varepsilon_0 \)), Maxwell calculated this speed to be approximately \( 3 \times 10^8 \) m/s, the experimentally measured speed of light. This led to the revolutionary conclusion that light is an electromagnetic wave. This speed is denoted by \( c \).

Plane Wave Solutions

A simple and important solution to the wave equation is a sinusoidal plane wave traveling in the x-direction. The electric and magnetic fields are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.

$$ \vec{E}(x,t) = E_m \cos(kx - \omega t) \hat{j} $$ $$ \vec{B}(x,t) = B_m \cos(kx - \omega t) \hat{k} $$

Here, \( E_m \) and \( B_m \) are the peak amplitudes, \( k = 2\pi/\lambda \) is the wave number, and \( \omega = 2\pi f \) is the angular frequency. The wave speed is \( c = \omega/k \).

From Maxwell's equations, a direct relationship between the amplitudes can be derived:

$$ E_m = c B_m $$

Energy and the Poynting Vector

Electromagnetic waves transport energy. The rate of energy flow per unit area is described by the Poynting vector, \( \vec{S} \).

$$ \vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\vec{E} \times \vec{B}) $$

The direction of \( \vec{S} \) is the direction of wave propagation, and its magnitude represents the instantaneous intensity (power per unit area) of the wave.

Energy Density

The total energy density \( u \) (energy per unit volume) in an electromagnetic field is the sum of the energy densities of the electric field (\( u_E \)) and the magnetic field (\( u_B \)).

$$ u_E = \frac{1}{2} \varepsilon_0 E^2 \quad \text{and} \quad u_B = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0} $$

For an electromagnetic wave, the energy is shared equally between the electric and magnetic fields, so \( u_E = u_B \).

Wave Intensity

The intensity \( I \) of a wave is the time-averaged magnitude of the Poynting vector, \( S_{avg} \). For a sinusoidal wave, this is:

$$ I = S_{avg} = \frac{E_m B_m}{2\mu_0} = \frac{E_m^2}{2\mu_0 c} $$

This is often referred to as the wave intensity.
