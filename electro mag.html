Physics Notes: Electromagnetism and AC Circuits

Vector Operators

The first two pages of this unit introduce some notations and their properties associated with vector algebra. Readers are encouraged to read any electromagnetism book and try to prove the properties.

Del Operator (∇)

In vector algebra, the vector differential operator, denoted by ∇ (Del), is defined as:

$$ \vec{\nabla} = \hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z} $$

Gradient, Divergence, and Curl

  • Gradient (Grad): The gradient of a scalar field \( \phi \) is a vector field.
    $$ \nabla \phi = \text{Grad} \, \phi $$
  • Divergence (Div): The divergence of a vector field \( \vec{A} \) is a scalar field.
    $$ \nabla \cdot \vec{A} = \text{Div} \, \vec{A} $$
  • Curl: The curl of a vector field \( \vec{A} \) is another vector field.
    $$ \nabla \times \vec{A} = \text{Curl} \, \vec{A} $$

Example: Gradient

Consider a scalar field \( \phi = x + y \). Then its gradient is:

$$ \nabla \phi = \hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x+y) + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x+y) + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(x+y) $$ $$ \nabla \phi = \hat{i}(1) + \hat{j}(1) + \hat{k}(0) = \hat{i} + \hat{j} $$

Divergence of a Vector

For a vector \( \vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k} \), the divergence is:

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{A} = \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial z} $$

Curl of a Vector

The curl of a vector \( \vec{A} \) can be expressed as a determinant:

$$ \nabla \times \vec{A} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \end{vmatrix} $$

For example, if \( \vec{A} = x \hat{i} + y \hat{j} + z \hat{k} \):

$$ \nabla \times \vec{A} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ x & y & z \end{vmatrix} = \hat{i}(0-0) - \hat{j}(0-0) + \hat{k}(0-0) = 0 $$

Properties and Identities

  • \( \nabla \cdot (\vec{A} + \vec{B}) = \nabla \cdot \vec{A} + \nabla \cdot \vec{B} \)
  • \( \nabla \cdot (k\vec{A}) = k(\nabla \cdot \vec{A}) \) (for scalar k)
  • \( \nabla \times (\vec{A} + \vec{B}) = \nabla \times \vec{A} + \nabla \times \vec{B} \)
  • \( \nabla \times (k\vec{A}) = k(\nabla \times \vec{A}) \) (for scalar k)
  • Divergence of Curl is zero: \( \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{A}) = 0 \)
  • Curl of Gradient is zero: \( \nabla \times (\nabla \phi) = 0 \)

Integral Theorems

Gauss's Divergence Theorem

This theorem relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the divergence of the field in the volume enclosed. It converts a surface integral into a volume integral.

$$ \oint_S \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{s} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{A}) dV $$

Stoke's Theorem

This theorem states that the line integral of a vector field \( \vec{A} \) around a closed curve C is equal to the surface integral of the curl of \( \vec{A} \) over any surface S bounded by C. It converts a line integral to a surface integral.

$$ \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{s} $$

The Classification of Vector Fields

Vector fields can be classified into four kinds based on their curl and divergence.

1. Lamellar (Irrotational) Field

Characterized by zero curl. It can be expressed as the gradient of a scalar potential \( \phi \).

$$ \nabla \times \vec{F} = 0 \quad \implies \quad \vec{F} = -\nabla \phi $$

2. Solenoidal Field

Characterized by zero divergence, which means there are no sources or sinks. It can be expressed as the curl of a vector potential \( \vec{A} \).

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{F} = 0 \quad \implies \quad \vec{F} = \nabla \times \vec{A} $$

Helmholtz's Theorem and The Four Kinds of Fields

A vector field \( \vec{F} \) is uniquely determined by its divergence and curl. It can be expressed as the sum of a lamellar field (gradient of a scalar potential) and a solenoidal field (curl of a vector potential).

$$ \vec{F} = -\nabla \phi + \nabla \times \vec{A} $$

Electromagnetic Theory: Maxwell's Equations

Maxwell's equations are a set of four fundamental equations that form the foundation of classical electromagnetism, classical optics, and electric circuits.

1. Gauss's Law for Electricity

Relates the net electric flux through a closed surface to the net electric charge enclosed within that surface.

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} $$

2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism

States that there are no magnetic monopoles. The net magnetic flux out of any closed surface is zero.

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 $$

3. Faraday's Law of Induction

A changing magnetic field induces an electromotive force (e.m.f), which creates a curling electric field.

$$ \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} $$

4. Ampere-Maxwell's Law

Magnetic fields are generated by electric currents and by changing electric fields (displacement current).

$$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $$

Equation of Continuity

This equation expresses the principle of local charge conservation.

$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 $$

It means that the change in charge density \( \rho \) in a volume is balanced by the net current \( \vec{J} \) flowing out of its surface.

Electromagnetic Waves

In a vacuum (where \( \rho = 0, \vec{J} = 0 \)), Maxwell's equations can be combined to form wave equations for the electric and magnetic fields.

The Wave Equation

Taking the curl of Faraday's Law and using the vector identity \( \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) - \nabla^2 \vec{E} \), we arrive at the wave equation for \( \vec{E} \):

$$ \nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} $$

A similar equation exists for the magnetic field \( \vec{B} \):

$$ \nabla^2 \vec{B} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{B}}{\partial t^2} $$

The speed of these waves is given by:

$$ c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0}} $$

Plane Wave Solution

A simple solution to the wave equation is a sinusoidal plane wave traveling in the x-direction:

$$ \vec{E} = \vec{E}_m \cos(kx - \omega t) \quad \text{and} \quad \vec{B} = \vec{B}_m \cos(kx - \omega t) $$

From Maxwell's equations, it can be shown that the magnitudes of the fields are related by:

$$ \frac{E_m}{B_m} = c $$

Energy and the Poynting Vector

Electromagnetic waves carry energy. The rate of energy flow per unit area is given by the Poynting vector \( \vec{S} \):

$$ \vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\vec{E} \times \vec{B}) $$

The energy densities of the electric (\( u_E \)) and magnetic (\( u_B \)) fields are equal in an EM wave:

$$ u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 \quad \text{and} \quad u_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2 \quad \implies \quad u_E = u_B $$

The time-averaged intensity \( I \) of the wave is the average magnitude of the Poynting vector:

$$ I = S_{avg} = \frac{E_m B_m}{2\mu_0} = \frac{E_m^2}{2\mu_0 c} = \frac{c B_m^2}{2\mu_0} $$

AC Theory

Basic Definitions

Alternating Current (AC) is described by sinusoidal functions of time.

  • Voltage: \( V(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t) \)
  • Current: \( I(t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t + \phi) \)

Here, \( V_0 \) and \( I_0 \) are the peak amplitudes, \( \omega = 2\pi f \) is the angular frequency, and \( \phi \) is the phase angle between voltage and current.

Average and RMS Values

  • Average Value (full cycle): The average of a sine wave over a full cycle is 0.
  • Average Value (half cycle): \( I_{avg} = \frac{2I_0}{\pi} \approx 0.637 I_0 \)
  • Root Mean Square (RMS) Value: The effective value of an AC current or voltage. It's the equivalent DC value that would produce the same heating effect.
    $$ I_{rms} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707 I_0 $$
    $$ V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.707 V_0 $$
  • Form Factor: The ratio of the RMS value to the average value (over a half-cycle).
    $$ \text{Form Factor} = \frac{I_{rms}}{I_{avg}} = \frac{I_0/\sqrt{2}}{2I_0/\pi} = \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}} \approx 1.11 $$

AC Circuits

The behavior of resistors, capacitors, and inductors in AC circuits is described by their impedance.

Impedance (Z)

Impedance is the complex generalization of resistance for AC circuits. \( Z = R + jX \), where R is resistance and X is reactance.

Components in AC Circuits

  • Resistor (R):

    Voltage and current are in phase (\(\phi = 0\)).

    $$ Z_R = R $$
  • Inductor (L):

    Voltage leads the current by 90° (\(\phi = \pi/2\)). The opposition to current is called inductive reactance \( X_L \).

    $$ Z_L = j\omega L \quad \implies \quad X_L = \omega L $$
  • Capacitor (C):

    Current leads the voltage by 90° (\(\phi = -\pi/2\)). The opposition to current is called capacitive reactance \( X_C \).

    $$ Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} = -j\frac{1}{\omega C} \quad \implies \quad X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} $$

The Series RLC Circuit

For a resistor, inductor, and capacitor connected in series, the total impedance is:

$$ Z = Z_R + Z_L + Z_C = R + j\omega L - j\frac{1}{\omega C} = R + j\left(X_L - X_C\right) $$

The magnitude of the impedance is:

$$ |Z| = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} $$

The phase angle \( \phi \) between the voltage and current is:

$$ \tan(\phi) = \frac{X_L - X_C}{R} $$

Resonance in a Series RLC Circuit

Resonance occurs when the inductive and capacitive reactances cancel each other out (\( X_L = X_C \)). At this point, the impedance is at its minimum (\( Z = R \)), and the current is at its maximum.

The resonant angular frequency \( \omega_0 \) is:

$$ \omega L = \frac{1}{\omega C} \quad \implies \quad \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} $$

At resonance, the current is in phase with the voltage (\( \phi = 0 \)).

Quality Factor (Q)

The Q factor is a measure of the "sharpness" of the resonance peak. A high Q factor indicates a narrow bandwidth and high selectivity.

$$ Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C R} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} $$

At resonance, the voltage across the inductor and capacitor can be much larger than the source voltage, a phenomenon called voltage magnification:

$$ V_L = V_C = Q \cdot V_{\text{source}} $$

Power in AC Circuits

The instantaneous power is \( P(t) = V(t)I(t) \). The average power dissipated in an AC circuit depends on the phase angle \( \phi \) and is given by:

$$ P_{avg} = V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos(\phi) $$

The term \( \cos(\phi) \) is called the power factor. Power is only dissipated in the resistor, so the average power can also be written as:

$$ P_{avg} = I_{rms}^2 R $$
