Part 3: Applications of Electromagnetic Waves

Analysis of a Given EM Wave

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q1c): The magnetic field of an electromagnetic wave is described as \( \vec{B} = B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t) \hat{j} \).
(i) What is the wave length (\(\lambda\)) of the wave?
(ii) Write an expression for the electric field (\(\vec{E}\)) associated with this wave.
(iii) What is the direction and the magnitude of the Poynting vector associated with this wave?

Solution

(i) Wavelength (\(\lambda\)): The wavelength is related to the wave number \( k \) by the formula \( k = 2\pi / \lambda \). Therefore, if we knew the value of \( k \), we could find the wavelength.
$$ \lambda = \frac{2\pi}{k} $$
The question does not provide a value for k, so the answer is in terms of k.
(ii) Electric Field (\(\vec{E}\)):

Direction: The direction of propagation is given by the argument \( (kx - \omega t) \), which indicates travel in the +x direction. The Poynting vector \( \vec{S} \propto \vec{E} \times \vec{B} \) must point in the direction of propagation (\( \hat{i} \)). We are given \( \vec{B} \) is in the \( \hat{j} \) direction. For \( \vec{E} \times \vec{B} \) to be in the \( \hat{i} \) direction, \( \vec{E} \) must be in the \( -\hat{k} \) direction, since \( (-\hat{k}) \times \hat{j} = \hat{i} \).

Magnitude: The amplitudes are related by \( E_0 = c B_0 \).

Expression: The electric field must be in phase with the magnetic field.

$$ \vec{E} = -E_0 \sin(kx - \omega t) \hat{k} = -c B_0 \sin(kx - \omega t) \hat{k} $$
(iii) Poynting Vector (\(\vec{S}\)):

Direction: As determined above, the direction of propagation is the +x direction (\(\hat{i}\)).

Magnitude: The instantaneous magnitude is \( S = \frac{EB}{\mu_0} \). The time-averaged magnitude (intensity) is what is usually meant.

$$ I = \langle S \rangle = \frac{E_0 B_0}{2\mu_0} = \frac{(c B_0) B_0}{2\mu_0} = \frac{c B_0^2}{2\mu_0} $$

Solar Constant

Question (Nov/Dec 2023, Q1c): At the upper surface of the Earth's atmosphere, the time-averaged magnitude of the Poynting vector \( \langle S \rangle = 1.35 \times 10^3 \) W/m² is referred to as the solar constant. (i) Assuming the Sun's radiation is a plane sinusoidal wave, obtain the magnitudes of the electric and magnetic fields.

Solution

Step 1: Find Electric Field Amplitude (\(E_m\)): Use the intensity formula. $$ I = \langle S \rangle = \frac{E_m^2}{2\mu_0 c} $$ $$ E_m = \sqrt{2\mu_0 c I} = \sqrt{2 (4\pi \times 10^{-7}) (3.0 \times 10^8) (1.35 \times 10^3)} $$ $$ E_m = \sqrt{1017.9 \times 10^3} \approx 1009 \text{ V/m} $$
Step 2: Find Magnetic Field Amplitude (\(B_m\)): Use the relationship \( E_m = c B_m \). $$ B_m = \frac{E_m}{c} = \frac{1009 \text{ V/m}}{3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} \approx 3.36 \times 10^{-6} \text{ T} $$
$$ E_m \approx 1009 \text{ V/m} \quad \text{and} \quad B_m \approx 3.36 \text{ µT} $$

Standing Waves

Question (Nov/Dec 2023, Q1d): Calculate the intensity of the standing wave given by \(E_y(x,t) = 2E_0\cos(kx)\cos(\omega t)\) and \(B_z(x,t) = 2B_0\sin(kx)\sin(\omega t)\). Hence, prove that the energy carried by the two waves... cancel each other with no net energy transfer.

Solution

Step 1: Calculate the Poynting Vector \( \vec{S} \): The wave propagates in the x-direction. $$ \vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}(\vec{E} \times \vec{B}) = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (E_y \hat{j} \times B_z \hat{k}) = \frac{E_y B_z}{\mu_0} \hat{i} $$ $$ S_x = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (2E_0\cos(kx)\cos(\omega t))(2B_0\sin(kx)\sin(\omega t)) $$ $$ S_x = \frac{4E_0 B_0}{\mu_0} \cos(kx)\sin(kx)\cos(\omega t)\sin(\omega t) $$
Step 2: Simplify using trigonometric identities: \( \sin(2\alpha)=2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha \). $$ S_x = \frac{E_0 B_0}{\mu_0} (2\cos(kx)\sin(kx))(2\cos(\omega t)\sin(\omega t)) $$ $$ S_x = \frac{E_0 B_0}{\mu_0} \sin(2kx) \sin(2\omega t) $$
Step 3: Calculate the Intensity (Time-Averaged Poynting Vector): We need to find the average of \( S_x \) over one period. $$ I = \langle S_x \rangle = \left\langle \frac{E_0 B_0}{\mu_0} \sin(2kx) \sin(2\omega t) \right\rangle $$ The term \( \sin(2kx) \) is constant with respect to time. The average of \( \sin(2\omega t) \) over one full cycle is zero. $$ \langle \sin(2\omega t) \rangle = 0 $$
$$ I = \langle S_x \rangle = \frac{E_0 B_0}{\mu_0} \sin(2kx) \times 0 = 0 $$
Conclusion: Since the time-averaged Poynting vector (intensity) is zero at all points in space, there is no net energy transfer over a cycle. Energy is stored in the E and B fields and sloshes back and forth between nodes and antinodes, but it does not propagate.

EMF in a Loop Antenna

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q1d): Find the ratio of the emf generated in a loop antenna by a television wave (100 MHz) to that generated by a radio wave (1 MHz) if both have equal electric field intensities.

Solution

Step 1: Relate EMF to Changing Magnetic Flux: According to Faraday's Law of Induction, the induced EMF (\(\mathcal{E}\)) in a loop is given by the rate of change of magnetic flux through it. $$ \mathcal{E} = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -A \frac{dB}{dt} $$ where A is the area of the loop.
Step 2: Express B in terms of time: For an EM wave, \( B(t) = B_m \cos(\omega t) \). $$ \frac{dB}{dt} = - \omega B_m \sin(\omega t) $$ The maximum EMF is \( \mathcal{E}_m = A \omega B_m \).
Step 3: Relate Bm to Em: We know \( B_m = E_m / c \). The question states that \( E_m \) is the same for both waves. $$ \mathcal{E}_m = A \omega \frac{E_m}{c} $$
Step 4: Form the ratio: The EMF is proportional to the angular frequency \( \omega \) (or frequency \( f \), since \( \omega = 2\pi f \)). All other terms (A, Em, c) are constant. $$ \frac{\mathcal{E}_{\text{TV}}}{\mathcal{E}_{\text{Radio}}} = \frac{A \omega_{\text{TV}} (E_m/c)}{A \omega_{\text{Radio}} (E_m/c)} = \frac{\omega_{\text{TV}}}{\omega_{\text{Radio}}} = \frac{f_{\text{TV}}}{f_{\text{Radio}}} $$
Step 5: Calculate the final value. $$ \frac{\mathcal{E}_{\text{TV}}}{\mathcal{E}_{\text{Radio}}} = \frac{100 \text{ MHz}}{1 \text{ MHz}} = 100 $$
The ratio of the EMFs is 100.
