Part 1: Foundations - Vector Calculus & Maxwell's Equations

Stoke's Theorem and its Application

Short Notes: Stoke's Theorem

Stoke's theorem relates a surface integral of the curl of a vector field to the line integral of that vector field around the boundary of the surface. It essentially says that the total "rotation" on a surface is equal to the "circulation" around its edge.

$$ \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{S} $$
Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q3a): (i) State Stoke's theorem. (ii) Using Stoke's theorem, prove that the curl of the gradient of a scalar (\(\Phi\)) is zero.

Solution

(i) Statement of Stoke's Theorem: The line integral of a vector field \( \vec{A} \) around a closed path \( C \) is equal to the surface integral of the curl of \( \vec{A} \) over any open surface \( S \) bounded by the path \( C \).
(ii) Proof that \( \nabla \times (\nabla \Phi) = 0 \):

Step 1: Let a vector field \( \vec{A} \) be the gradient of a scalar potential \( \Phi \), so \( \vec{A} = \nabla \Phi \).

Step 2: The line integral of a gradient field around a closed path is always zero because the start and end points are the same (\(\Phi(a) - \Phi(a) = 0\)).

$$ \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \oint_C (\nabla \Phi) \cdot d\vec{l} = 0 $$

Step 3: Apply Stoke's theorem to this result: \( \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{S} \).

$$ 0 = \int_S (\nabla \times (\nabla \Phi)) \cdot d\vec{S} $$

Step 4: Since this equation must hold true for any arbitrary surface \( S \), the integrand itself must be zero everywhere.

$$ \nabla \times (\nabla \Phi) = 0 $$

Maxwell's Equations

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q3b.i & Nov/Dec 2023, Q3a.i): Write the Maxwell's equations and explain the significance of each equation with the use of integral forms.

Solution: Maxwell's Equations and Their Significance

  1. Gauss's Law for Electricity: \( \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{S} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0} \)
    Significance: Electric charges create electric fields. The net flux out of a closed surface is proportional to the net charge enclosed.
  2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism: \( \oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S} = 0 \)
    Significance: There are no magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops.
  3. Faraday's Law of Induction: \( \oint_C \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \)
    Significance: A changing magnetic flux induces an electromotive force (EMF) and a curling electric field. This is the basis for generators.
  4. Ampere-Maxwell Law: \( \oint_C \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} \)
    Significance: A magnetic field is created by electric currents (\(I_{enc}\)) and by changing electric fields (the "displacement current" term). This unification is key to the existence of EM waves.

The Ampere-Maxwell Law and Displacement Current

Question (Nov/Dec 2023, Q3a.ii): Discuss the limitations of the Ampere's equation and justify the additional term by Maxwell...

Solution

Limitation of Ampere's Original Law (\( \oint \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} \)): It fails for non-steady currents, such as in a charging capacitor. It violates the law of conservation of charge (the continuity equation \( \nabla \cdot \vec{J} = -\partial\rho/\partial t \)) because taking the divergence of Ampere's law gives \( \nabla \cdot \vec{J} = 0 \), which is only true for steady currents.
Justification of Maxwell's Term: Maxwell proposed that a changing electric field (\(\partial\vec{E}/\partial t\)) in the gap of a capacitor creates a magnetic field just like a real current. This "displacement current" (\(I_d = \varepsilon_0 d\Phi_E/dt\)) restores consistency with charge conservation and completes the theory, predicting EM waves.
Condition for Vanishing Contribution: Maxwell's term vanishes when the electric field is static (\( \partial \vec{E}/\partial t = 0 \)), which is the condition for magnetostatics. In this case, the law reduces to Ampere's original law.

Magnetic Monopoles

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q3b.iii): Suppose magnetic monopoles did exist. What form would Maxwell's equations take?

Solution

If magnetic monopoles existed, they would act as sources of the magnetic field, making the equations for electricity and magnetism symmetric.

1. Gauss's Law for Magnetism: Would become non-zero, with \( \rho_m \) as the magnetic charge density.
$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = \mu_0 \rho_m $$
2. Faraday's Law: Would include a magnetic current term \( \vec{J}_m \).
$$ \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} - \mu_0 \vec{J}_m $$
