Part 3: Wave Applications (Ultra-Detailed)

EMF Induced in a Loop Antenna

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q1d): Find the ratio of the emf generated in a loop antenna by a television wave (100 MHz) to that generated by a radio wave (1 MHz) if both have equal electric field intensities.

Ultra-Detailed Solution

Step 1: Apply Faraday's Law of Induction.

The electromotive force (EMF), \( \mathcal{E} \), induced in a closed loop is the negative rate of change of magnetic flux \( \Phi_B \) through the loop.

$$ \mathcal{E}(t) = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} $$
Step 2: Define Magnetic Flux for a Plane Wave.

For a loop of area A, where the magnetic field \( \vec{B} \) is uniform over the area and perpendicular to the plane of the loop, the flux is \( \Phi_B = B \cdot A \). The magnetic field of the wave varies sinusoidally with time: \( B(t) = B_m \cos(\omega t) \).

$$ \Phi_B(t) = A B_m \cos(\omega t) $$
Step 3: Differentiate the Flux to find the EMF. $$ \mathcal{E}(t) = - \frac{d}{dt} [A B_m \cos(\omega t)] = - A B_m \frac{d}{dt}[\cos(\omega t)] $$

Using the chain rule, this becomes:

$$ \mathcal{E}(t) = - A B_m [-\omega \sin(\omega t)] = A \omega B_m \sin(\omega t) $$
Step 4: Identify the Peak (Maximum) EMF.

The maximum value of \( \sin(\omega t) \) is 1. Therefore, the peak EMF, \( \mathcal{E}_m \), is:

$$ \mathcal{E}_m = A \omega B_m $$
Step 5: Relate the Magnetic Field Amplitude to the Electric Field Amplitude.

For an EM wave, \( E_m = c B_m \), so \( B_m = E_m / c \). Substitute this into the EMF equation:

$$ \mathcal{E}_m = A \omega \frac{E_m}{c} $$
Step 6: Form the Ratio for the Two Waves.

Let TV denote the television wave and Radio denote the radio wave. The question states they have the same loop area (A) and the same electric field intensity (\( E_m \)). The speed of light c is also constant.

$$ \frac{\mathcal{E}_{m, \text{TV}}}{\mathcal{E}_{m, \text{Radio}}} = \frac{A \omega_{\text{TV}} (E_m/c)}{A \omega_{\text{Radio}} (E_m/c)} $$

Cancel the common terms \( A, E_m, c \):

$$ \frac{\mathcal{E}_{m, \text{TV}}}{\mathcal{E}_{m, \text{Radio}}} = \frac{\omega_{\text{TV}}}{\omega_{\text{Radio}}} $$
Step 7: Substitute Frequencies.

Since angular frequency \( \omega = 2\pi f \), the ratio of angular frequencies is the same as the ratio of frequencies.

$$ \frac{\mathcal{E}_{m, \text{TV}}}{\mathcal{E}_{m, \text{Radio}}} = \frac{2\pi f_{\text{TV}}}{2\pi f_{\text{Radio}}} = \frac{f_{\text{TV}}}{f_{\text{Radio}}} $$ $$ = \frac{100 \text{ MHz}}{1 \text{ MHz}} = 100 $$
The ratio of the induced EMF is 100.

Future Exam Preparation: Model Question

Question: A standing electromagnetic wave is formed by two counter-propagating waves. The resulting fields are \(E_y(x,t) = 2E_0\cos(kx)\cos(\omega t)\) and \(B_z(x,t) = 2B_0\sin(kx)\sin(\omega t)\). Calculate the instantaneous electric and magnetic energy densities, \(u_E(x,t)\) and \(u_B(x,t)\). Show that the total energy density \(u_{total}(x,t)\) is not constant in time, implying that energy flows between points of pure E-field and pure B-field.

Ultra-Detailed Model Answer

Step 1: Write down the formulas for energy density.

Electric energy density: \( u_E = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 E^2 \)

Magnetic energy density: \( u_B = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} B^2 \)

Step 2: Calculate the instantaneous electric energy density \(u_E(x,t)\).

Substitute the given E-field into the formula.

$$ u_E(x,t) = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 [2E_0\cos(kx)\cos(\omega t)]^2 $$ $$ u_E(x,t) = \frac{1}{2}\varepsilon_0 (4E_0^2 \cos^2(kx) \cos^2(\omega t)) = 2\varepsilon_0 E_0^2 \cos^2(kx) \cos^2(\omega t) $$
Step 3: Calculate the instantaneous magnetic energy density \(u_B(x,t)\).

Substitute the given B-field into the formula.

$$ u_B(x,t) = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} [2B_0\sin(kx)\sin(\omega t)]^2 $$ $$ u_B(x,t) = \frac{1}{2\mu_0} (4B_0^2 \sin^2(kx) \sin^2(\omega t)) = \frac{2B_0^2}{\mu_0} \sin^2(kx) \sin^2(\omega t) $$
Step 4: Simplify \(u_B\) using \( E_0 = cB_0 \) and \( c^2 = 1/(\mu_0\varepsilon_0) \).

From these, \( B_0 = E_0/c \), so \( B_0^2 = E_0^2/c^2 = E_0^2 \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \).

$$ u_B(x,t) = \frac{2(E_0^2 \mu_0 \varepsilon_0)}{\mu_0} \sin^2(kx) \sin^2(\omega t) = 2\varepsilon_0 E_0^2 \sin^2(kx) \sin^2(\omega t) $$
Step 5: Write the total energy density \(u_{total} = u_E + u_B \). $$ u_{total}(x,t) = 2\varepsilon_0 E_0^2 [\cos^2(kx) \cos^2(\omega t) + \sin^2(kx) \sin^2(\omega t)] $$
Step 6: Analyze the result to show energy flow.

The total energy density is a function of both position (x) and time (t). It is not constant.

  • At an E-field antinode (where \( \cos(kx) = \pm 1, \sin(kx) = 0 \)), the energy is purely electric: \( u_{total} = 2\varepsilon_0 E_0^2 \cos^2(\omega t) \). It oscillates between a maximum and zero.
  • At a B-field antinode (where \( \sin(kx) = \pm 1, \cos(kx) = 0 \)), the energy is purely magnetic: \( u_{total} = 2\varepsilon_0 E_0^2 \sin^2(\omega t) \). It also oscillates.

When the energy at the E-field antinode is maximum (\(\cos^2(\omega t)=1\)), the energy at the B-field antinode is zero (\(\sin^2(\omega t)=0\)). A quarter cycle later, the situation is reversed. This shows that energy is not propagating along the x-axis, but is instead "sloshing" back and forth between points of high electric field and high magnetic field. This confirms the conclusion from the Poynting vector calculation that there is no net energy transfer.
