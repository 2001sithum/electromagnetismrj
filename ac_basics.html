Part 4: AC Theory & Basic Components

AC Waveforms and Definitions

In AC (Alternating Current) circuits, voltage and current vary sinusoidally with time.

  • Voltage: \( v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t) \)
  • Current: \( i(t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t + \phi) \)

Where \( V_0 \) and \( I_0 \) are the peak amplitudes, \( \omega = 2\pi f \) is the angular frequency, and \( \phi \) is the phase angle, representing the time shift between the voltage and current waveforms.

Average and RMS Values

Since the average value of a sine wave over a full cycle is zero, we need a more useful measure for its effective value. This is the Root Mean Square (RMS) value.

Derivation of the RMS Value

The RMS value is the DC equivalent current that would deliver the same average power to a resistor. It is found by taking the square Root of the Mean of the Square of the current.

Step 1 (Square): Square the current waveform. $$ i(t)^2 = (I_0 \sin(\omega t))^2 = I_0^2 \sin^2(\omega t) $$
Step 2 (Mean): Find the average (mean) value of \( i(t)^2 \) over one full period \( T \). $$ \langle i^2 \rangle = \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T I_0^2 \sin^2(\omega t) \, dt $$ Use the trigonometric identity \( \sin^2(x) = \frac{1 - \cos(2x)}{2} \). $$ \langle i^2 \rangle = \frac{I_0^2}{T} \int_0^T \frac{1 - \cos(2\omega t)}{2} \, dt $$ $$ = \frac{I_0^2}{2T} \left[ \int_0^T dt - \int_0^T \cos(2\omega t) \, dt \right] $$ The integral of \( \cos(2\omega t) \) over a full period is zero. $$ = \frac{I_0^2}{2T} [t]_0^T = \frac{I_0^2}{2T} (T - 0) = \frac{I_0^2}{2} $$
Step 3 (Root): Take the square root of the result.
$$ I_{rms} = \sqrt{\langle i^2 \rangle} = \sqrt{\frac{I_0^2}{2}} = \frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}} $$
Similarly, for voltage: \( V_{rms} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}} \).

AC Behavior of Circuit Components

We analyze how each basic component behaves in an AC circuit using the concept of impedance (Z), which is the AC equivalent of resistance.

1. The Resistor

In a purely resistive circuit, the current is always in phase with the voltage (\( \phi = 0 \)).

According to Ohm's law: \( v(t) = i(t)R \). If \( v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t) \), then:

$$ i(t) = \frac{V_0}{R} \sin(\omega t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t) $$

The impedance of a resistor is simply its resistance, \( Z_R = R \).

2. The Inductor

In a purely inductive circuit, the voltage leads the current by 90° (\( \phi = +\pi/2 \)). An inductor opposes changes in current.

The voltage across an inductor is \( v(t) = L \frac{di}{dt} \). If \( i(t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t) \), then:

$$ v(t) = L \frac{d}{dt} (I_0 \sin(\omega t)) = \omega L I_0 \cos(\omega t) = (\omega L I_0) \sin(\omega t + \pi/2) $$

The peak voltage is \( V_0 = I_0 (\omega L) \). The quantity \( \omega L \) is the opposition to current flow and is called inductive reactance, \( X_L \).

$$ X_L = \omega L $$

The impedance of an inductor is \( Z_L = jX_L = j\omega L \), where \( j = \sqrt{-1} \).

3. The Capacitor

In a purely capacitive circuit, the current leads the voltage by 90° (\( \phi = -\pi/2 \)). A capacitor opposes changes in voltage.

The charge on a capacitor is \( q(t) = C v(t) \), and current is \( i(t) = \frac{dq}{dt} \). If \( v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t) \), then:

$$ i(t) = \frac{d}{dt} (C V_0 \sin(\omega t)) = \omega C V_0 \cos(\omega t) = (\omega C V_0) \sin(\omega t + \pi/2) $$

The peak current is \( I_0 = V_0 (\omega C) \), or \( V_0 = I_0 \left(\frac{1}{\omega C}\right) \). The quantity \( \frac{1}{\omega C} \) is the opposition to current flow and is called capacitive reactance, \( X_C \).

$$ X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C} $$

The impedance of a capacitor is \( Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} = -j X_C \).
