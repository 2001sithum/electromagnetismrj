Part 5: RLC Circuits & Resonance

The Series RLC Circuit

When a resistor (R), inductor (L), and capacitor (C) are connected in series to an AC voltage source, their combined opposition to the current is called impedance (Z). The voltage drops across L and C are 180° out of phase and thus subtract from each other.

Derivation of Impedance and Phase Angle

We use a phasor diagram, where the current phasor \( I_0 \) is aligned with the x-axis.

Step 1: The voltage phasors are:
  • \( V_R = I_0 R \) (in phase with current)
  • \( V_L = I_0 X_L \) (leads current by 90°)
  • \( V_C = I_0 X_C \) (lags current by 90°)
Step 2: The total voltage \( V_0 \) is the vector sum of these individual voltages. The horizontal component is \( V_R \) and the vertical component is \( V_L - V_C \).
Step 3: Use the Pythagorean theorem to find the magnitude of the total voltage. $$ V_0^2 = V_R^2 + (V_L - V_C)^2 $$ $$ V_0^2 = (I_0 R)^2 + (I_0 X_L - I_0 X_C)^2 $$ $$ V_0^2 = I_0^2 [R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2] $$
Step 4: The magnitude of the total impedance \( Z \) is defined as \( V_0 / I_0 \).
$$ Z = \frac{V_0}{I_0} = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} $$
Step 5: The phase angle \( \phi \) is the angle between the total voltage phasor and the current phasor.
$$ \tan(\phi) = \frac{V_L - V_C}{V_R} = \frac{I_0(X_L - X_C)}{I_0 R} = \frac{X_L - X_C}{R} $$

Resonance

Resonance is a special condition in an RLC circuit that occurs when the inductive reactance equals the capacitive reactance (\( X_L = X_C \)). At this frequency, the impedance is at its minimum value, and the current is at its maximum.

Derivation of the Resonant Frequency

Step 1: Set the condition for resonance. $$ X_L = X_C $$
Step 2: Substitute the expressions for the reactances. $$ \omega_0 L = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C} $$ Where \( \omega_0 \) is the resonant angular frequency.
Step 3: Solve for \( \omega_0 \). $$ \omega_0^2 = \frac{1}{LC} $$
$$ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} $$

At resonance:

  • Impedance is minimum: \( Z = R \).
  • Current is maximum: \( I_{max} = V_0 / R \).
  • Phase angle is zero: \( \phi = 0 \). The circuit behaves purely resistively.

Quality Factor (Q)

The Quality Factor (Q) of a resonant circuit is a dimensionless parameter that describes how underdamped an oscillator or resonator is. It is a measure of the "sharpness" of the resonance peak.

A higher Q factor means a narrower bandwidth, indicating the circuit is more selective to a specific frequency.

$$ Q = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0 C R} = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} $$

At resonance, the voltages across the inductor and capacitor can be much larger than the source voltage. This is called voltage magnification.

$$ V_L = I_{max} X_L = \left(\frac{V_0}{R}\right) (\omega_0 L) = Q V_0 $$ $$ V_C = I_{max} X_C = \left(\frac{V_0}{R}\right) \left(\frac{1}{\omega_0 C}\right) = Q V_0 $$

Power in AC Circuits

In an AC circuit, power is only dissipated by the resistor. The instantaneous power varies with time. The more useful quantity is the average power, \( P_{avg} \).

Derivation of Average Power

Step 1: Write the expressions for instantaneous voltage and current. $$ v(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t) $$ $$ i(t) = I_0 \sin(\omega t - \phi) $$
Step 2: The instantaneous power is \( p(t) = v(t)i(t) \). $$ p(t) = V_0 I_0 \sin(\omega t) \sin(\omega t - \phi) $$
Step 3: Use the trigonometric identity \( \sin(A)\sin(B) = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(A-B) - \cos(A+B)] \). $$ p(t) = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2} [\cos(\phi) - \cos(2\omega t - \phi)] $$
Step 4: To find the average power, we average \( p(t) \) over one period. The average of the \( \cos(2\omega t - \phi) \) term over a full cycle is zero. $$ P_{avg} = \langle p(t) \rangle = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2} \cos(\phi) $$
Step 5: Express this in terms of RMS values, since \( V_{rms} = V_0/\sqrt{2} \) and \( I_{rms} = I_0/\sqrt{2} \). $$ P_{avg} = \left(\frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \left(\frac{I_0}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \cos(\phi) $$
$$ P_{avg} = V_{rms} I_{rms} \cos(\phi) $$

The term \( \cos(\phi) \) is called the power factor. It ranges from 0 to 1 and represents the fraction of the total apparent power that is dissipated as real power.
