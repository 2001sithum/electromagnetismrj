Part 2: Maxwell's Equations and the Equation of Continuity

Maxwell's equations are the four cornerstones of classical electromagnetism. They describe how electric and magnetic fields are generated and altered by each other and by charges and currents.

1. Gauss's Law for Electricity

This law states that the net electric flux out of a closed surface is proportional to the total electric charge enclosed within that surface.

Integral Form:

$$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\varepsilon_0} $$

This form is useful for calculating the electric field in situations with high symmetry.

Derivation of the Differential Form

Step 1: Express the enclosed charge \( Q_{enc} \) as a volume integral of the charge density \( \rho \). $$ Q_{enc} = \int_V \rho \, dV $$ So, Gauss's Law becomes: $$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_0} \int_V \rho \, dV $$
Step 2: Apply the Divergence Theorem to the left side to convert the surface integral into a volume integral. $$ \oint_S \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{s} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) \, dV $$
Step 3: Equate the two volume integrals. $$ \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) \, dV = \int_V \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \, dV $$
Step 4: Since this equality must hold for any arbitrary volume \( V \), the integrands themselves must be equal.
$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} $$
This is the differential form, which tells us that electric field lines diverge from positive charges and converge on negative charges.

2. Gauss's Law for Magnetism

This law states that there are no magnetic monopoles. Magnetic field lines always form closed loops; they never start or end at a point. Therefore, the net magnetic flux through any closed surface is always zero.

Integral Form:

$$ \oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{s} = 0 $$

Derivation of the Differential Form

Step 1: Apply the Divergence Theorem to the integral form. $$ \oint_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{s} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{B}) \, dV $$
Step 2: Since the integral form is equal to zero, we have: $$ \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{B}) \, dV = 0 $$
Step 3: As this must hold for any volume \( V \), the integrand must be zero.
$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 $$
This is the differential form. It mathematically states that the magnetic field is "solenoidal," meaning it has no sources or sinks.

3. Faraday's Law of Induction

This law describes how a time-varying magnetic field creates (induces) an electric field. The line integral of the electric field around a closed loop (the electromotive force, or EMF) is equal to the negative rate of change of the magnetic flux through the surface enclosed by the loop.

Integral Form:

$$ \oint_C \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = - \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = - \frac{d}{dt} \int_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{s} $$

Derivation of the Differential Form

Step 1: Apply Stoke's Theorem to the left side to convert the line integral into a surface integral. $$ \oint_C \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{E}) \cdot d\vec{s} $$
Step 2: Bring the time derivative inside the integral on the right side (as a partial derivative, since \( \vec{B} \) can also vary with position). $$ - \frac{d}{dt} \int_S \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{s} = - \int_S \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\vec{s} $$
Step 3: Equate the two surface integrals. $$ \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{E}) \cdot d\vec{s} = - \int_S \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \cdot d\vec{s} $$
Step 4: Since this must be true for any surface \( S \), the integrands must be equal.
$$ \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} $$
This is the differential form, which shows that a changing magnetic field creates a "curling" electric field.

4. Ampere-Maxwell Law

This law describes how magnetic fields are generated. Originally, Ampere's Law stated that magnetic fields were only created by electric currents (\( \vec{J} \)). Maxwell added a crucial second term, the "displacement current," showing that a time-varying electric field also creates a magnetic field.

Integral Form:

$$ \oint_C \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \mu_0 I_{enc} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} $$

Derivation of the Differential Form

Step 1: Apply Stoke's Theorem to the left side. $$ \oint_C \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{s} $$
Step 2: Express the enclosed current \( I_{enc} \) and electric flux \( \Phi_E \) as surface integrals. $$ I_{enc} = \int_S \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{s} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{d\Phi_E}{dt} = \int_S \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \cdot d\vec{s} $$
Step 3: Substitute these into the integral form and combine terms. $$ \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{s} = \mu_0 \int_S \vec{J} \cdot d\vec{s} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \int_S \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \cdot d\vec{s} $$ $$ \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{B}) \cdot d\vec{s} = \int_S \left( \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \right) \cdot d\vec{s} $$
Step 4: For any surface \( S \), the integrands must be equal.
$$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $$
This is the differential form, showing that magnetic fields are created by both moving charges and changing electric fields.

The Equation of Continuity (Charge Conservation)

This is not one of Maxwell's four equations, but it is a fundamental principle that is consistent with them. It states that electric charge is conserved locally.

Derivation

Step 1: Start with the Ampere-Maxwell Law. $$ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} $$
Step 2: Take the divergence of both sides. $$ \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{B}) = \nabla \cdot \left( \mu_0 \vec{J} + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \right) $$
Step 3: The divergence of a curl is always zero (\( \nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{B}) = 0 \)). $$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \nabla \cdot \left( \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \right) $$ $$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) $$
Step 4: Substitute Gauss's Law (\( \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \rho / \varepsilon_0 \)). $$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0} \right) $$ $$ 0 = \mu_0 (\nabla \cdot \vec{J}) + \mu_0 \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} $$
Step 5: Divide by \( \mu_0 \) to get the final equation.
$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{J} + \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = 0 $$
This means that the rate at which charge density \( \rho \) decreases in a volume is equal to the rate at which current density \( \vec{J} \) flows out of it.
