Part 2: Theory of EM Waves & The Poynting Vector

Deriving the Wave Equation

Short Notes: From Maxwell to Waves

A key prediction of Maxwell's equations is the existence of electromagnetic waves. In free space (no charges or currents), a changing E-field creates a B-field, which in turn creates an E-field. This self-sustaining propagation is an EM wave. The wave equation can be derived directly from Maxwell's curl equations.

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q3b.ii & Nov/Dec 2023, Q3b.ii): How do Maxwell's equations imply that electromagnetic waves exist in free space and that light is such a wave? / Obtain the wave equation for \( \vec{E} \).

Solution: Derivation of the Wave Equation for \( \vec{E} \)

Step 1: Start with Maxwell's two curl equations in free space (\( \vec{J}=0, \rho=0 \)).

\( \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \)   (Faraday's Law)

\( \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \)   (Ampere-Maxwell Law)

Step 2: Take the curl of Faraday's Law. $$ \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = \nabla \times \left(- \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}\right) = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \times \vec{B}) $$
Step 3: Use the vector identity \( \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{A}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{A}) - \nabla^2\vec{A} \) on the left side. $$ \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) - \nabla^2\vec{E} = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \times \vec{B}) $$
Step 4: In free space, \( \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0 \). Substitute this and the Ampere-Maxwell Law into the equation. $$ \nabla(0) - \nabla^2\vec{E} = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left(\mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}\right) $$ $$ - \nabla^2\vec{E} = - \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} $$
Step 5: The result is the wave equation for the electric field.
$$ \nabla^2\vec{E} = \mu_0 \varepsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} $$
Implication: This equation is a standard wave equation of the form \( \nabla^2\psi = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2\psi}{\partial t^2} \). By comparison, the speed of these waves is \( v = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0 \varepsilon_0} \). Plugging in the values for \( \mu_0 \) and \( \varepsilon_0 \) gives \( v \approx 3.0 \times 10^8 \) m/s, which is the measured speed of light, c. This proved that light is an electromagnetic wave.

Relationship between E and B Fields in an EM Wave

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q1a & Nov/Dec 2023, Q1a): Show that at a given instant the ratio of the electric field to the magnetic field of an electromagnetic wave equals the speed of light. i.e., E/B = c.

Solution: Proof that E/B = c

Step 1: Assume a sinusoidal plane wave traveling in the x-direction. The fields are transverse and mutually perpendicular.

\( \vec{E}(x,t) = E_m \cos(kx - \omega t) \hat{j} \)

\( \vec{B}(x,t) = B_m \cos(kx - \omega t) \hat{k} \)

Step 2: Apply Faraday's Law in differential form: \( \nabla \times \vec{E} = - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \).
Step 3: Calculate the left-hand side (LHS): $$ \nabla \times \vec{E} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ 0 & E_m \cos(kx-\omega t) & 0 \end{vmatrix} = \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(E_m \cos(kx-\omega t)) = -k E_m \sin(kx-\omega t) \hat{k} $$
Step 4: Calculate the right-hand side (RHS): $$ - \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial}{\partial t}(B_m \cos(kx-\omega t) \hat{k}) = - B_m (-\omega \sin(kx-\omega t)) \hat{k} = \omega B_m \sin(kx-\omega t) \hat{k} $$
Step 5: Equate the magnitudes of the LHS and RHS. $$ k E_m = \omega B_m $$
Step 6: Rearrange the equation. Since the wave speed \( c = \omega/k \), we get the result. $$ \frac{E_m}{B_m} = \frac{\omega}{k} = c $$ Since this holds for the amplitudes (Em, Bm), it also holds for the instantaneous magnitudes (E, B) at any point.
$$ \frac{E}{B} = c $$

Intensity of an Electromagnetic Wave

Short Notes: The Poynting Vector

The Poynting vector \( \vec{S} \) describes the direction and magnitude of energy flow in an EM field. Its magnitude is the power per unit area (intensity), and its direction is the direction of wave propagation.

$$ \vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\vec{E} \times \vec{B}) $$

For a sinusoidal wave, the intensity \( I \) is the time-average of the magnitude of the Poynting vector, \( I = \langle S \rangle \).

Question (Jan/Feb 2023, Q1b & Nov/Dec 2023, Q1b): Prove that the intensity (or time-averaged Poynting vector) of an electromagnetic wave is given by \( I = \frac{E_m^2}{2\mu_0 c} \).

Solution: Derivation of Wave Intensity

Step 1: For a plane wave with \( \vec{E} \) and \( \vec{B} \) perpendicular, the magnitude of the Poynting vector is: $$ S = \frac{1}{\mu_0} EB \sin(90^\circ) = \frac{EB}{\mu_0} $$
Step 2: Substitute \( B = E/c \) into the equation. $$ S = \frac{E(E/c)}{\mu_0} = \frac{E^2}{\mu_0 c} $$
Step 3: The intensity \( I \) is the average value of S over one cycle. We substitute \( E = E_m \cos(kx - \omega t) \). $$ I = \langle S \rangle = \left\langle \frac{E_m^2 \cos^2(kx - \omega t)}{\mu_0 c} \right\rangle $$
Step 4: The average value of \( \cos^2(\theta) \) over a full cycle is \( 1/2 \). $$ \langle \cos^2(kx - \omega t) \rangle = \frac{1}{2} $$
Step 5: Therefore, the intensity is:
$$ I = \frac{E_m^2}{2\mu_0 c} $$
