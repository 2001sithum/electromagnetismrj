Part 5: AC Circuit Applications (Ultra-Detailed)

Average Power in an RLC Circuit

Question (Nov/Dec 2023, Q2a.ii): Provide a full derivation for the average power delivered to a series RLC circuit, \( P_{av} = \frac{V_{rms}^2 R \omega^2}{R^2 \omega^2 + L^2(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2} \).

Ultra-Detailed Derivation

Step 1: Start with the fundamental definition of average power.

Power is only dissipated in the resistor. The average power is given by:

$$ P_{av} = I_{rms}^2 R $$
Step 2: Express RMS current in terms of RMS voltage and impedance.

From the AC version of Ohm's Law, \( I_{rms} = V_{rms} / Z \). Substitute this into the power equation.

$$ P_{av} = \left(\frac{V_{rms}}{Z}\right)^2 R = \frac{V_{rms}^2 R}{Z^2} $$
Step 3: Write out the full expression for impedance squared, \(Z^2\). $$ Z^2 = R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2 = R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)^2 $$
Step 4: Substitute the resonance condition to eliminate C.

The resonant frequency is \( \omega_0^2 = 1/LC \). We can rearrange this to express C in terms of L and \( \omega_0 \): \( C = 1/(\omega_0^2 L) \).

$$ Z^2 = R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega (1/(\omega_0^2 L))}\right)^2 = R^2 + \left(\omega L - \frac{\omega_0^2 L}{\omega}\right)^2 $$
Step 5: Factor and simplify the impedance expression.

Factor out L from the parenthesis and find a common denominator.

$$ Z^2 = R^2 + \left(L \left(\omega - \frac{\omega_0^2}{\omega}\right)\right)^2 = R^2 + L^2 \left(\frac{\omega^2 - \omega_0^2}{\omega}\right)^2 $$ $$ Z^2 = R^2 + \frac{L^2(\omega^2 - \omega_0^2)^2}{\omega^2} $$ This is a key intermediate result.
Step 6: Substitute the detailed \(Z^2\) back into the power equation. $$ P_{av} = \frac{V_{rms}^2 R}{R^2 + \frac{L^2(\omega^2 - \omega_0^2)^2}{\omega^2}} $$
Step 7: Final algebraic manipulation.

To get the desired form, find a common denominator in the bottom part of the fraction, which is \( \omega^2 \).

$$ P_{av} = \frac{V_{rms}^2 R}{\frac{R^2\omega^2 + L^2(\omega^2 - \omega_0^2)^2}{\omega^2}} $$

Now, multiply the numerator by the reciprocal of the denominator.

$$ P_{av} = \frac{V_{rms}^2 R \omega^2}{R^2 \omega^2 + L^2(\omega^2 - \omega_0^2)^2} $$

This expression is particularly useful for analyzing the power delivered to the circuit as the driving frequency \( \omega \) is varied relative to the resonant frequency \( \omega_0 \).

Future Exam Preparation: Model Question

Question: For a series RLC circuit, the sharpness of the resonance is described by the Quality Factor, Q. The bandwidth, \( \Delta\omega \), is the range of frequencies over which the power delivered to the circuit is at least half of the maximum power. Prove that \( Q = \omega_0 / \Delta\omega \).

Ultra-Detailed Model Answer

1. Define the half-power condition.

Maximum power \( P_{max} \) occurs at resonance (\( \omega = \omega_0 \)), where \( P_{max} = V_{rms}^2/R \). The half-power points (\( \omega_1, \omega_2 \)) are the frequencies where \( P_{av} = P_{max}/2 \).

$$ \frac{V_{rms}^2 R}{Z^2} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{V_{rms}^2}{R} \implies Z^2 = 2R^2 $$
2. Solve the impedance equation.

\( R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2 = 2R^2 \implies (X_L - X_C)^2 = R^2 \). Taking the square root gives two solutions:

$$ X_L - X_C = \pm R $$
3. Find the two half-power frequencies.

Let's solve \( \omega L - 1/(\omega C) = R \). Multiply by \( \omega \) and rearrange into a quadratic equation for \( \omega \):

$$ \omega^2 L - \omega R - 1/C = 0 \implies \omega = \frac{R \pm \sqrt{R^2 + 4L/C}}{2L} $$

The physically meaningful solution (positive frequency) is \( \omega_2 = \frac{R + \sqrt{R^2 + 4L/C}}{2L} \). Similarly, solving \( \omega L - 1/(\omega C) = -R \) gives \( \omega_1 = \frac{-R + \sqrt{R^2 + 4L/C}}{2L} \).

4. Calculate the bandwidth \( \Delta\omega \). $$ \Delta\omega = \omega_2 - \omega_1 = \left( \frac{R + \sqrt{\dots}}{2L} \right) - \left( \frac{-R + \sqrt{\dots}}{2L} \right) = \frac{2R}{2L} = \frac{R}{L} $$
5. Relate this to the Q-factor.

The Q-factor is defined as \( Q = \omega_0 L / R \). We can rearrange our bandwidth result as \( R/L = 1/\Delta\omega \). Let's see. No, that's not right. Rearrange the Q-factor definition: \( R/L = \omega_0 / Q \).

Now equate the two expressions for \( R/L \):

$$ \Delta\omega = \frac{R}{L} = \frac{\omega_0}{Q} $$
6. Final Proof.

Rearranging the equation from the previous step gives the desired relationship:

$$ Q = \frac{\omega_0}{\Delta\omega} $$

This proves that a high-Q circuit has a small bandwidth, making it highly selective, while a low-Q circuit has a wide bandwidth.
