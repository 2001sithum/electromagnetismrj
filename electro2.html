Part 1: Vector Operators & Integral Theorems

The Del Operator (âˆ‡)

In vector calculus, the Del operator, denoted by \( \nabla \), is a fundamental vector differential operator. It is not a vector in itself, but it acts on scalar or vector fields to produce results like the gradient, divergence, and curl.

In Cartesian coordinates, it is defined as:

$$ \vec{\nabla} = \hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z} $$

Here, \( \hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k} \) are the unit vectors along the x, y, and z axes, respectively.

The Three Fundamental Operations

1. Gradient of a Scalar Field (\( \nabla \phi \))

When \( \nabla \) acts on a scalar field \( \phi(x, y, z) \), it produces a vector field called the gradient. This vector points in the direction of the greatest rate of increase of the scalar field, and its magnitude is that rate of increase.

Derivation

Step 1: Start with the Del operator and a scalar field \( \phi \). $$ \vec{\nabla} = \hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z} $$
Step 2: Apply the operator to \( \phi \) by distributing the partial derivatives. $$ \nabla \phi = \left(\hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right) \phi $$
Result: This gives the gradient vector.
$$ \nabla \phi = \hat{i} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} $$

Example: If \( \phi = x + y \), then \( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} = 1 \), \( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial y} = 1 \), and \( \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial z} = 0 \). Therefore, \( \nabla \phi = \hat{i} + \hat{j} \).

2. Divergence of a Vector Field (\( \nabla \cdot \vec{A} \))

The dot product of \( \nabla \) with a vector field \( \vec{A}(x, y, z) \) produces a scalar field called the divergence. It measures the magnitude of a vector field's source or sink at a given point. A positive divergence indicates a source (flux flowing out), while a negative divergence indicates a sink (flux flowing in).

Derivation

Step 1: Define the Del operator and a vector field \( \vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k} \).
Step 2: Take the dot product. Remember that \( \hat{i} \cdot \hat{i} = 1 \), \( \hat{i} \cdot \hat{j} = 0 \), etc. $$ \nabla \cdot \vec{A} = \left(\hat{i} \frac{\partial}{\partial x} + \hat{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial y} + \hat{k} \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right) \cdot (A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k}) $$
Result: The terms with non-matching unit vectors become zero.
$$ \nabla \cdot \vec{A} = \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial z} $$

3. Curl of a Vector Field (\( \nabla \times \vec{A} \))

The cross product of \( \nabla \) with a vector field \( \vec{A} \) produces another vector field called the curl. The curl measures the tendency of the vector field to rotate or circulate around a point. The direction of the curl vector is given by the right-hand rule.

Derivation (using determinant form)

Step 1: The cross product can be conveniently expressed as the determinant of a matrix. $$ \nabla \times \vec{A} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \end{vmatrix} $$
Step 2: Expand the determinant. $$ = \hat{i} \left(\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial z}\right) - \hat{j} \left(\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial z}\right) + \hat{k} \left(\frac{\partial A_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial y}\right) $$
Result: The final expression for the curl vector.
$$ \nabla \times \vec{A} = \left(\frac{\partial A_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial A_y}{\partial z}\right)\hat{i} + \left(\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial x}\right)\hat{j} + \left(\frac{\partial A_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial y}\right)\hat{k} $$

Example: If \( \vec{A} = x\hat{i} + y\hat{j} + z\hat{k} \), all partial derivatives like \( \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial y} \) are zero, so \( \nabla \times \vec{A} = 0 \). This field has no rotation.

Fundamental Integral Theorems

These theorems are crucial for relating the differential forms of physical laws (like Maxwell's equations) to their integral forms, which are often easier to measure experimentally.

Gauss's Divergence Theorem

This theorem relates the total flux of a vector field \( \vec{A} \) out of a closed surface \( S \) to the integral of the divergence of the field over the volume \( V \) enclosed by that surface.

In essence, it states that the total outward flow from a volume is the sum of all the sources minus the sinks inside it.

$$ \oint_S \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{s} = \int_V (\nabla \cdot \vec{A}) dV $$

This theorem allows us to convert a surface integral into a volume integral, which is a key step in deriving the differential form of Gauss's Law for electricity.

Stoke's Theorem

This theorem relates the line integral of a vector field \( \vec{A} \) around a closed loop \( C \) to the integral of the curl of the field over any open surface \( S \) bounded by that loop.

In essence, it states that the total circulation around a loop is the sum of all the tiny circulations (curls) on the surface enclosed by the loop.

$$ \oint_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{s} $$

This theorem is essential for deriving the differential forms of Faraday's Law and Ampere's Law.
