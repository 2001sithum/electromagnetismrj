Phase Relationship in an Inductor

Model Question (Implied): Provide a full derivation explaining why "the voltage across an inductor leads the current by \( \pi/2 \)".

Ultra-Detailed Explanation and Derivation

Physical Intuition: An inductor opposes changes in current due to Lenz's Law (it generates a back-EMF). For a current to start flowing and increase, a voltage must be applied *first* to overcome this opposition. Therefore, the voltage waveform must lead the current waveform in time.
Mathematical Derivation:

1. Start with the fundamental relationship for an inductor: the voltage across it is proportional to the rate of change of current through it.

$$ v_L(t) = L \frac{di(t)}{dt} $$

2. To find the phase relationship, let's assume a sinusoidal current flows through the inductor:

$$ i(t) = I_m \sin(\omega t) $$

3. Differentiate the current to find the voltage:

$$ v_L(t) = L \frac{d}{dt}[I_m \sin(\omega t)] = L I_m \frac{d}{dt}[\sin(\omega t)] $$

4. Perform the differentiation using the chain rule:

$$ v_L(t) = L I_m [\cos(\omega t) \cdot \omega] = \omega L I_m \cos(\omega t) $$

5. The peak voltage is \( V_m = \omega L I_m \). So, \( v_L(t) = V_m \cos(\omega t) \).

6. To compare the phases, express both voltage and current using the same function (e.g., sine). Use the identity \( \cos(\theta) = \sin(\theta + \pi/2) \).

$$ v_L(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t + \pi/2) $$

7. Conclusion: Comparing \( i(t) = I_m \sin(\omega t) \) with \( v_L(t) = V_m \sin(\omega t + \pi/2) \), we see that the voltage's phase angle is \( \pi/2 \) ahead of the current's phase angle. This means the voltage leads the current by \( \pi/2 \).

RLC Series Impedance and Resonance

Model Question (Implied): Provide an ultra-detailed derivation of the impedance Z and phase angle \( \phi \) for a series RLC circuit.

Ultra-Detailed Derivation using Phasors

1. Set up the Phasor Diagram: In a series circuit, the current \( i(t) \) is the same through all components. We use its phasor, \( I_m \), as the reference, placing it along the positive x-axis.
2. Draw Voltage Phasors for Each Component:
  • Resistor: Voltage is in phase with current. So, \( V_{R,m} \) is also along the x-axis. Its length is \( I_m R \).
  • Inductor: Voltage leads current by 90°. So, \( V_{L,m} \) is along the positive y-axis. Its length is \( I_m X_L \).
  • Capacitor: Voltage lags current by 90°. So, \( V_{C,m} \) is along the negative y-axis. Its length is \( I_m X_C \).
3. Find the Total Voltage Phasor by Vector Addition:

The total source voltage phasor, \( V_{s,m} \), is the vector sum of the individual voltage phasors.

The total x-component is just \( V_{R,m} \).

The total y-component is the sum of the inductor and capacitor voltages: \( V_{L,m} - V_{C,m} \).

The total voltage phasor \( V_{s,m} \) is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with these two components as its sides.

4. Derive the Magnitude of the Impedance (Z):

Using the Pythagorean theorem on the phasor triangle:

$$ (V_{s,m})^2 = (V_{R,m})^2 + (V_{L,m} - V_{C,m})^2 $$

Substitute the Ohm's law equivalents for each peak voltage (\(V_m = I_m X\)):

$$ (V_{s,m})^2 = (I_m R)^2 + (I_m X_L - I_m X_C)^2 $$

Factor out the common \( I_m^2 \):

$$ (V_{s,m})^2 = I_m^2 [R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2] $$

Impedance Z is defined as the ratio of total peak voltage to total peak current, \( Z = V_{s,m} / I_m \). Rearrange the equation:

$$ \frac{(V_{s,m})^2}{I_m^2} = R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2 \implies Z^2 = R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2 $$
$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} $$
5. Derive the Phase Angle (\( \phi \)):

The phase angle \( \phi \) is the angle between the total voltage phasor \( V_{s,m} \) and the current phasor \( I_m \) (the x-axis). From the right triangle in the phasor diagram:

$$ \tan(\phi) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} = \frac{V_{L,m} - V_{C,m}}{V_{R,m}} $$

Substitute the Ohm's law equivalents:

$$ \tan(\phi) = \frac{I_m X_L - I_m X_C}{I_m R} = \frac{I_m (X_L - X_C)}{I_m R} $$
$$ \tan(\phi) = \frac{X_L - X_C}{R} $$
